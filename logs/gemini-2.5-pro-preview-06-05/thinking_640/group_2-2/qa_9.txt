### Step-by-Step Reasoning

The temperature of a region is determined by several key geographical and climatic factors. Let's compare the Arctic and Antarctica on each of these points.

**Step 1: Land vs. Water**

*   **Antarctica:** Is a continent—a massive landmass covered by a giant ice sheet. Land loses heat much more quickly than water.
*   **The Arctic:** Is an ocean (the Arctic Ocean) surrounded by continents. The vast body of water, even when covered by ice, acts as a heat reservoir, retaining heat from the summer and moderating the winter temperatures. The water temperature rarely drops below -2°C (28°F), the freezing point of saltwater. This relatively "warm" water radiates heat upwards, warming the air above it.

**Conclusion of Step 1:** The landmass of Antarctica allows it to get much colder than the ocean-based Arctic.

**Step 2: Elevation**

*   **Antarctica:** Is the highest continent on Earth, with an average elevation of about 2,500 meters (8,200 feet). The South Pole itself sits at an elevation of roughly 2,835 meters (9,300 feet). Higher altitudes are significantly colder.
*   **The Arctic:** Is mostly at sea level. The Arctic Ocean's surface is, by definition, at sea level, and much of the surrounding land is low-lying tundra.

**Conclusion of Step 2:** Antarctica's high average elevation makes it inherently colder than the sea-level Arctic.

**Step 3: Isolation by Ocean and Air Currents**

*   **Antarctica:** Is surrounded by the Antarctic Circumpolar Current, the strongest ocean current on the planet. This current flows unimpeded around the continent, effectively creating a barrier that blocks warmer ocean waters from the subtropics from reaching the coast. A strong, stable wind pattern called the Polar Vortex also isolates the continent's air, keeping it frigid.
*   **The Arctic:** Is not as isolated. Warm ocean currents, like the North Atlantic Drift (an extension of the Gulf Stream), flow into the Arctic Ocean, bringing significant heat. Weather patterns from the south also penetrate the Arctic more easily, bringing warmer air.

**Conclusion of Step 3:** Antarctica is climatically isolated, which keeps it colder, while the Arctic receives heat from the rest of the globe.

**Step 4: The Ice Sheets**

*   **Antarctica:** Is covered by a colossal ice sheet that is, on average, 2.1 km (1.3 miles) thick and up to 4.8 km (3 miles) thick in some places. This immense sheet of white ice is highly reflective (high albedo), bouncing the sun's energy back into space very effectively.
*   **The Arctic:** Has a much thinner layer of sea ice, typically only a few meters thick. While it is also reflective, there is far less total ice volume, and in the summer, large areas become open water, which absorbs solar radiation instead of reflecting it.

**Conclusion of Step 4:** Antarctica's massive, permanent ice sheet reflects more solar energy and contributes to its high elevation, keeping it colder than the Arctic with its thinner, seasonal sea ice.
