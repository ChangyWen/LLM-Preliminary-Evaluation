### Step-by-Step Reasoning

This is different from a **sidereal day**, which is the time it takes for a planet to complete one full 360-degree rotation on its axis relative to the distant stars. For our daily experience, the solar day is what matters.

**Step 2: Analyze a day on Earth.**
*   **Earth's Rotation (Sidereal Day):** It takes Earth approximately 23 hours and 56 minutes to complete one full rotation.
*   **Earth's Day-Night Cycle (Solar Day):** Because Earth is also moving along its orbit around the Sun, it has to rotate a little bit extra each day to "catch up" to the Sun. This brings the total time for a solar day to an average of **24 hours**.

**Step 3: Analyze a day on Venus.**
Venus is very unusual. It has two key characteristics that make its day extremely long:
1.  **Extremely Slow Rotation:** Venus rotates on its axis incredibly slowly. A single sidereal day (one full 360° rotation) on Venus takes about **243 Earth days**.
2.  **Retrograde Rotation:** Venus rotates backward (east to west) compared to Earth and most other planets in our solar system.

Because of this slow, backward rotation, the solar day on Venus is very different from its rotation period. The time from one sunrise to the next on Venus is about **117 Earth days**.
