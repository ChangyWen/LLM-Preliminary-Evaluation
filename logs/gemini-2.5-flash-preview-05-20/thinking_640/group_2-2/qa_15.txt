No, the speed of sound is **not** faster than the speed of light.

Here's the step-by-step reasoning process:

**Step 1: Understand the Nature and Typical Speed of Light**
*   **What it is:** Light is an electromagnetic wave. It does not require a medium to travel and can propagate through a vacuum.
*   **Its Speed:** The speed of light in a vacuum (denoted as 'c') is a fundamental physical constant.
    *   **Value:** Approximately **299,792,458 meters per second (m/s)**.
    *   This is roughly **300,000 kilometers per second (km/s)** or **186,000 miles per second (miles/s)**.
*   **Key Characteristic:** According to Einstein's theory of relativity, the speed of light in a vacuum is the absolute maximum speed at which all energy, matter, and information can travel.

**Step 2: Understand the Nature and Typical Speed of Sound**
*   **What it is:** Sound is a mechanical wave. It is a vibration that travels through a medium (like air, water, or solids) by causing particles in that medium to oscillate. It *cannot* travel through a vacuum.
*   **Its Speed:** The speed of sound varies significantly depending on the medium it's traveling through and its temperature, density, and elasticity.
    *   **In Air (at 20°C or 68°F):** Approximately **343 meters per second (m/s)**.
    *   This is roughly **1,235 kilometers per hour (km/h)** or **767 miles per hour (mph)**.
    *   **In Water:** Around 1,500 m/s.
    *   **In Steel:** Around 5,100 m/s.
*   **Key Characteristic:** Sound requires a medium to propagate. If there's no medium (like in the vacuum of space), there's no sound.

**Step 3: Compare the Speeds**
*   **Speed of Light (in vacuum):** ~300,000,000 m/s
*   **Speed of Sound (in air):** ~343 m/s

When you compare these numbers, it's clear that 300,000,000 m/s is vastly greater than 343 m/s. Even in denser media where sound travels faster (like steel at 5,100 m/s), it is still orders of magnitude slower than the speed of light.

*   You see the lightning (light) almost instantaneously.
*   You hear the thunder (sound) seconds later, because the sound travels much slower than the light. The delay allows you to estimate how far away the storm is.
